Participant Information and Consent Form

Name of Project: How do predators decide which prey to attack based on prior experience?

You are invited to participate in a study of predator-prey relationships. The aim of this study is to understand how a predator's previous encounters with prey affect responses to future prey encounters. We have chosen to use frogs as prey species since they naturally vary in antipredator strategies.

The study is being conducted by Mr Eric Guerra-Grenier (eric.guerra-grenier@mail.mcgill.ca) and Prof David M. Green (david.m.green@mcgill.ca) from the Redpath Museum - McGill University, as well as Dr James B. Barnett (barnettj@mcmaster.ca) from the Department of Psychology, Neuroscience and Behaviour - McMaster University.

Mr Eric Guerra-Grenier is a PhD candidate and this research is to be part of his doctoral thesis under the supervision of Prof David M. Green, Redpath Museum.

If you decide to participate, you will be asked to view a number of images of frogs and tell us if you think they are toxic or not. You will be asked to score a total of 21 images, and the task will take less than 5 minutes. From your responses we will obtain the frequency with which each image is considered to be toxic as well as the time it took each participant to score each image.

Any information or personal details gathered in the course of the study are confidential, except as required by law. No individual will be identified in any publication of the results.
